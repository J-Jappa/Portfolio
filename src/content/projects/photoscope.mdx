This one is different from the rest of my portfolio. It isn't aerodynamics or machine learning; it's a **for-the-love-of-it design project**, and it's deliberately tucked away. There's no nav link to it. The only way in is a small, dim `PHOTO//SCOPE` chip in the site footer. If you've found this write-up, consider the map already half-drawn.

The short version: I shoot film, and I wanted somewhere to put those photos that felt like _the photos_, not another neat white gallery. So I built an interactive, retro-futurist terminal for them. A wireframe Earth you can spin, with every location I've shot pinned to the globe. Click a pin and a little archive viewer boots up. There's a CRT contact sheet, camera models you can rotate, comets, scanlines and a start-up sequence. It leans hard into Star Wars readouts, Tron, Evangelion warning screens and old NASA/JPL mission terminals.

## Where it started

The whole thing crept up on me. It began as a plain idea: give my photography its own page, styled differently to the engineering side of the site. The first version was an ordinary responsive gallery, auto-built from folders, with location and film stock on hover.

Then I had a small thought that turned into a rabbit hole: what if, instead of a grid, it was a **map**? A light outline of the world on white, a dot everywhere I'd taken a photo, and clicking a dot opened that location's roll.

I built that. It was nice. It was also, I decided about a day later, far too tasteful. So I asked the obvious follow-up: what if it were retro-futurist instead? Star Wars terminals? Tron? The map quietly became a **3D spinning wireframe globe**, and the project stopped being a gallery and became a whole little world.

From there it was pure iteration on feel. Fewer polygons for a chunkier vector look. A low-poly lat/long cage, neon land outlines, a dotted starfield. Then **comets** (blue, red and yellow) with long trails that pass _behind_ the Earth and occlude correctly. A CRT bulge like a real curved tube. Dense scanlines. Chromatic aberration on the lines but never on the photos. A boot sequence with green phosphor text. A terminal photo viewer with a decrypt-style reveal and three little **RGB histograms** read live off each image. A condensed serif for the headings. An amber contact sheet view to switch into. It kept accreting details, and that was the fun of it.

## The cameras

The detail I'm fondest of: each roll knows **which camera shot it**, and the interface shows a live wireframe of that camera, spinning, that you can grab and orbit. It's a nod to the "CRAFT" model panels in old mission-control mock-ups. There's a little camera archive you can open to flick through all of them with specs and notes.

They're the four bodies I actually use: a Nikon FM, a Yashica-A TLR, a Pentax Espio 95s point-and-shoot, and a 3D-printed Panomicron Oxygen panoramic. They're currently chunky low-poly _placeholders_ that I'm modelling properly over time, and they render with the exact same hand-rolled projection as the globe, so the whole thing shares one visual language.

## How it actually works

The stack is intentionally boring so the _output_ can be loud:

- **Astro**, statically generated. No framework on the client beyond a couple of vanilla scripts.
- The globe, comets, camera models and starfield all render on a **plain 2D `<canvas>`**, using an orthographic projection written by hand (with a lot of help, more on that below). No WebGL, no three.js. Land outlines come from decimated Natural Earth coastline data.
- **Content is just folders.** Every roll lives in a folder named `Place, Country · Film Stock · Camera`. A build-time scan (`import.meta.glob`) discovers the images, `astro:assets` optimises them to WebP (TIFFs included), and `exifr` reads the capture date. Add a roll and it shows up on the globe, the contact sheet and the carousel, with its pin placed and its metadata attached. Nothing to wire up by hand.
- The CRT look is layered CSS and canvas: static scanlines, a barrel warp on the HUD, a vignette, and a filter-based chromatic split, kept off the photographs themselves so the actual images stay clean.

Two things worth flagging: the dates are film **scan/edit** dates rather than true capture dates (that metadata doesn't survive a lab scanner), and the camera models are stylised stand-ins for now.

## Built with Claude Code

Here's the part I want to be upfront about: **I'm a mechanical / ML engineer, not a web developer.** A year ago I could not have built this, and I still couldn't write most of it unaided.

Almost all of the code here was written by **pair-programming with Claude Code**. My half was the part I _can_ do: taste and direction. I brought the photos, the references (a Tron / Eva / JPL moodboard in my head), and a running list of what to change next. Claude turned those into the parts I can't write myself, from the orthographic projection and the comet occlusion test to the fix for the scanlines rendering wrong on the deployed build.

It was genuinely collaborative, and occasionally comic. More than once we chased a bug that only appeared on the live site down through device-pixel-ratio and GPU compositing, or rebuilt an animation after I realised it had drifted from what I'd sketched. The takeaway for me, as an engineer, is less "AI wrote my website" and more that **describing intent precisely**, the same skill behind a good spec or a good research question, is most of what it now takes to build well outside your own discipline.
