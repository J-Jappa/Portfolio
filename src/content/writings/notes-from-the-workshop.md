A workshop is an honest place. The material does not care about your intentions, only your tolerances. Here are a few things it taught me that I keep coming back to.

## Measure the thing you have, not the thing you drew

The drawing is a wish. The part is a fact. When they disagree — and they will — believe the part. Most of the frustration I have watched people carry into a build comes from arguing with reality instead of measuring it.

## Fixtures are worth the delay

Every hour spent making a jig feels like an hour stolen from the "real" work. It almost never is. A good fixture turns a skilled operation into a repeatable one, and repeatable is what lets you stop holding your breath.

## Finish is a decision, not an afterthought

The difference between a part that looks made and one that looks *thrown together* is usually a few minutes at the very end — a chamfer, a wipe of the edge, a straight label. It reads as care because it is care.

---

None of this is profound. That is rather the point. The small disciplines are the ones that compound.
